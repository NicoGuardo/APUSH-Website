APUSH Review Timeline



What is going on?
Basically a timeline explaining a lot of the major things that happened in every time period.
If the panel is marked with an (*) asterisk, then it can be expanded.


Early-1762

Christoper Columbus "discovers" America in August of 1492

Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)*

The Treaty of Tordesillas was between Portugal and Spain, and it declared that newly discovered lands to the west of an imaginary line in the Atlantic Ocean would belong to Spain and newly discovered lands to the east of the line would belong to Portugal.

Founding of Jamestown in 1607

Navigation Acts start being passed by Parliament in 1651

Treaty of Utrecht (1713)*

The Treaty of Utrecht ended Queen Ann's War and transferred large areas of French territory in North America to the English, including Nova Scotia and Newfoundland.

Great Awakening (1730s)*

The Great Awakening emphasized ritual, doctrine, and a more emotional attachment to religion. It stressed God as a creator of a perfectly ordered universe where one could learn about him through nature. It placed less emphasis on human sinfulness and damnation.

Stono Rebellion (1739)*

The Stono Rebellion was the most serious slave rebellion in the the colonial period in South Carolina. One hundred African Americans got weapons, killed several whites, then tried to escape to southern Florida. The uprising was crushed and the participants executed. The main form of rebellion was running away, though there was no where to go.
1763-1800

Salutary Neglect ends after Seven Years' War in 1763

Proclamation of 1763*

The Proclamation of 1763 from the British government forbade British colonists from settling west of the Appalacian Mountains and required any settlers already living west of the mountains to move back east.

Stamp Act Congress gathers in New York City in 1765

Boston Massacre on March 5, 1770

Boston Tea Party in Boston Harbor on December 16, 1773

First Continental Congress meets in Philadelphia in 1774

Declaration of Independence signed on July 4, 1774

Land Ordinance of 1785*

The Land Ordinance of 1785 ended the disputes between the states, created townships, expanded U.S. territory, and gave settlers land.

Northwest Ordinance of 1787*

The Northwest Ordinance of 1787 defined the process by which new states could be admitted into the Union from the Northwest Territory. The ordinance forbade slavery in the territory but allowed citizens to vote on the legality of slavery once statehood had been established. The Northwest Ordinance was the most lasting measure of the national government under the Articles of Confederation.

The Federalist Papers (1788)*

The Federalist papers were a collection of essays written by Alexander Hamilton, John Jay, and James Madison explaining how the new government and constitution would work. The purpose was to convince the New York state legislature to ratify the Constitution, which it did.

George Washington becomes first president on April 30, 1789

George Washington's Farewell Address (1796)*

The Farewell Address stressed that the U.S. should stay away from permanent alliances with foreign countries. Temporary alliances wouldn't be quite as dangerous, but even so then they should be made only in "extraordinary emergencies." Washington also spoke against partisan bitterness. This document was rejected by the Jeffersonians, who favored the alliance with France.

Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions (1798)*

The Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions were put into practice by Jefferson and Madison. These were secretly made to get the rights taken away with the Alien and Sedition Acts back. These also brought about the later compact theory which gave the states more power than the federal government.

XYZ Affair (1798)*

In the XYZ Affair, a commission had been sent to France in 1797 to discuss the disputes that had arisen out of the U.S. refusal to honor the Franco-American Treaty of 1778. Adams sent delegates to meet with French foreign minister Talleyrand in the hopes of working things out. Talleyrand's three agents told the American delegates that they could meet with Talleyrand only in exchange for a very large bribe. The Americans did not pay the bribe, and in 1798 Adams made the incident public, substituting the letters "X, Y and Z" for the names of the three French agents in his report to Congress.

Failed Gabriel's Rebellion in 1800

1801-1860

Thomas Jefferson buys Louisiana Purchase in 1803

Aaron Burr slays Alexander Hamilton in duel on July 11, 1804

Embargo Act of 1807*

The Embargo Act of 1807 forbade all exportation of goods from the United States. Britain and France had been continuously harassing the U.S. and seizing U.S. ships and men. Since the U.S. was not prepared to fight in a war, Jefferson hoped to weaken Britain and France by stopping trade. The act ended up hurting our economy more than theirs and was repealed in 1809. It eventually led to the War of 1812.

Robert Fulton directs the construction of steamboat in New York in 1807

Adams–Onís Treaty of 1819*

Also known as Transcontinental Treaty, the Adams–Onís Treaty of 1819 purchased Florida from Spain. It established a western boundary for U.S. and prevented Seminoles from invading Georgia.

Corrupt Bargain (1824)*

The Corrupt Bargain was an event during the 1824 presidential election where Henry Clay did behind-the-scenes work in order to secure the victory of John Quincy Adams over Andrew Jackson. This enraged Jackson supporters and would help him win in 1828.

Tariff of Abominations (1828)*

The Tariff of Abominations favored western agricultural interests by raising tariffs or import taxes on imported hemp, wool, fur, flax, and liquor, favoring Northern manufacturers. In the South, these tariffs raised the cost of manufactured goods, angering them and causing more sectionalist feelings.

Indian Removal Act of 1830*

The Indian Removal Act of 1830 authorized Andrew Jackson to negotiate land-exchange treaties with tribes living east of the Mississippi. The treaties enacted under this act's provisions paved the way for the reluctant (and often forcible) emigration of tens of thousands of American Indians to the West.

Panic of 1837*

State banks issued paper money and financed wild speculation, especially in federal lands. Jackson issued the Specie Circular to force the payment for federal lands with gold or silver, and many state banks collapsed as a result.

Trail of Tears (1838)*

The Trail of Tears was forced relocation of Cherokee Indians. They traveled from North Carolina and Georgia through Tennessee, Kentucky, Illinois, Missouri, and Arkansas (more than 800 miles) to the Indian Territory. More than 4,000 Cherokees died of cold, disease, and lack of food during the 116-day journey.

The Irish Potato Famine, also known as the Great Hunger, begins in 1845

Fugitive Slave Act (1850)*

The Fugitive Slave Act made it a crime to help runaway slaves. It allowed for the arrest of escaped slaves in areas where slavery was illegal and required their return to slaveholders. It was also part of the Compromise of 1850.

Uncle Tom's Cabin by Harriet Beecher Stowe published in 1852

John Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry in 1859

South Carolina secedes on December 20, 1860

1861-1920

Homestead Act of 1862*

The Homestead Act of 1862 reassured western migration by accommodating settlers with 160 acres of public land. In return, homesteaders paid a small fee and were required to complete five years of residence.

Pacific Railway Act of 1862*

The Pacific Railway Act of 1862 aided in the construction of a railroad and telegraph line from the Missouri River to the Pacific Ocean. It also secured the government's the use of the it for postal, military, and other purposes.

Sherman's March (1864)*

William Sherman campaigned his "March to the Sea" from Atlanta to South Carolina where he and his army applied a total warfare and scorched earth policy. It led to over a million dollars in damage and crushed the south.

Force Acts (1870)*

The government used the Force Acts to ban the use of terror, force, or bribery to prevent someone from voting because of their race. Other laws banned the KKK entirely and brought forth military help to enforce these laws.

Compromise of 1877*

The Compromise of 1877 withdrew federal soldiers from their remaining position in the South, enacted federal legislation that would spur industrialization in the South, appointed Democrats to patronage positions in the south, and appointed Rutherford B. Hayes as president.

Thomas Edison invents the light bulb in November 1879

Railroads create the first time zones on November 18, 1883

How the Other Half Lives by Jacob Riis published in 1890

U.S. Army massacres Sioux at Wounded Knee on December 29, 1890

George Waring appointed commissioner of the New York Street Cleaning in 1895

Hamill Brothers discover oil in Spindletop, Texas on January 10, 1901

Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire disaster on March 25, 1911

Henry Ford creates revolutionary Ford Model T car in Detroit in 1908

William Mulholland shows off Los Angeles Aqueduct on November 5, 1913

128 Americans die on sinking of Lusitania by German U-Boat on May 7, 1915

Chicago race riot leads to Red Summer in July of 1919

Palmer Raids (1920)*

The Palmer Raids were operations coordinated by Attorney General Mitchel Palmer in which federal marshals raided the homes of suspected radicals and the headquarters of radical organization in 32 cities.
1921-1980

Saint Valentine's Day Massacre on February 14, 1929

Black Tuesday: stock market crashes on October 29, 1929

Black Sunday: severe dust storm on April 14, 1935

Operation Barbarossa: Axis invasion of Soviet Union on June 22, 1941

Japanese attack on Pearl Harbor on December 7, 1941

D-Day Allied invasion of Normandy, France on June 6, 1944

Hiroshima bombed on August 6, 1945 and Nagasaki bombed on August 9, 1945

U-2 spy plane shot down while in Soviet airspace on May 1, 1960

Failed Bay of Pigs invasion of Cuba by CIA on April 17, 1961

Cuban Missile Crisis during October of 1962

Malcolm X is assassinated on February 21, 1965

Martin Luther King Jr. is assassinated on April 4, 1968

Woodstock Festival takes place in New York in August of 1969

Kent State University shootings of four unarmed students on May 4, 1970

Watergate incident takes place on June 17, 1972

Richard Nixon resigns on August 9, 1974

1981-Today

Space shuttle Challenger explodes after 73 seconds on January 28, 1986

USA Patriot Act of 2001*

The USA Patriot Act was passed due to the September 11 attacks. It sought to prevent further terrorist attacks by allowing greater government access to electronic communications and other information. It was criticized by some as violating civil liberties.
